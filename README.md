Privacy Policy Language Strategy for Nova Scotia Power

# Decision Statement 

Should Nova Scotia Power's Chief Privacy Officer revise the company's privacy policy using plain language principles or maintain current legalistic standards to minimize liability risk?

# Executive Summary

Privacy policies have evolved dramatically over the past decade, particularly following major regulatory interventions like the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) in 2018 and the California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA). Research demonstrates that privacy policies have more than doubled in length during this period, with median reading levels increasing substantially (Amos et al., 2021; Wagner, 2021). Paradoxically, while regulations mandate "clear and plain language," empirical studies show that policies have become more complex and formalized rather than more accessible (Gao et al., 2022). This suggests that regulatory pressure may inadvertently encourage legal defensiveness over consumer comprehension.

For Nova Scotia Power, this decision carries high stakes as a regulated monopoly providing essential services to over 500,000 customers. Unlike competitive markets where dissatisfied customers can switch providers, Nova Scotia Power operates under a contract requiring public trust. Research on critical infrastructure providers indicates that information asymmetry between firms and consumers is problematic in sectors with captive customer bases. When the most important sections of privacy policies are those describing data sharing practices and user rights, carry the greatest cognitive burden (Adhikari et al., 2023), customers cannot meaningfully evaluate how their data is handled, undermining informed consent and eroding trust.

The Chief Privacy Officer faces competing institutional pressures. Legal teams advocate for comprehensive, protective language to minimize liability risk, while customer-facing departments recognize that policies damage relationships and increase complaints. Recent psycholinguistic analyses reveal that vague statements in privacy policies are increasing while clear statements decline (Belcheva et al., 2023), and firms increasingly rely on "pacifying phrases" designed to alleviate privacy concerns without providing substantive information. This decision will determine whether Nova Scotia Power perpetuates these industry trends toward opacity or differentiates itself through accessible communication that rebuilds customer trust while managing legal risk through alternative strategies.

# Implications for the Decision

My analysis of 195 privacy policies from 2021 to 2026 shows a major gap between how companies talk and how much users actually engage with them. In sectors like Transportation (specifically Canada Post), the "Cognitive Complexity" scores are very high. This means the policies are written in a way that is very hard for a normal person to read. Despite this, people still use these services because they have to, not because they understand the terms. On the other hand, the Health sector (like Yukon Health) uses a more personal "Organizational Voice," which helps build a more stable relationship with the public.

The evidence suggests that the most promising path for any company is to increase their Organizational Voice score. Companies that use "we" and "us" instead of robotic legal terms see better interaction from their customers. The high-risk, or less promising, option is to stick with the current style found in Telecommunications. These companies have stayed "complex" for years, which suggests they aren't trying to make things easier for their users.

We still don't know for sure if people are engaging with complex policies because they trust the company or just because they don't have another choice. We also need to see if new laws in 2026 will actually force these companies to make their language simpler or if they will just keep using "legalese."
